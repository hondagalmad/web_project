Mercury

Basic Stats


Type: Terrestrial planet

Diameter (equatorial): ~4,879 km

Mass: ~0.055 × Earth’s mass

Moons: 0

Length of day: ≈ 58.6 Earth days

Length of year: ≈ 88 Earth days

Distance from Sun: ~57.9 million km (≈ 0.39 AU)

Average surface temperature: ~167 °C (day), ~−173 °C (night)

Aatmosphere & Surface Hell


Mercury has a very thin atmosphere called an exosphere, made mostly of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and potassium. It cannot support weather like on Earth. Mercury’s surface is rocky and heavily cratered, similar to the Moon, with large temperature swings between extremely hot days and freezing nights. There is no liquid or dense atmosphere

Exploration & Missions

Past & Current Missions

Mercury has been visited by a few spacecraft. NASA’s Mariner 10 flew by Mercury three times between 1974 and 1975, providing the first close-up images of the planet’s surface. Later, ESA and JAXA’s BepiColombo mission, launched in 2018, is currently en route to Mercury and will study the planet in detail once it arrives, observing its surface, magnetic field, and thin exosphere.

Upcoming Missions

The main upcoming mission to Mercury is ESA and JAXA’s BepiColombo, which launched in 2018 and is expected to arrive at Mercury in 2025. It will conduct detailed studies of the planet’s surface, magnetic field, exosphere, and internal structure, providing the most comprehensive data on Mercury to date.

Weird Facts

  • Mercury has the most extreme temperature swings in the solar system, from about 430 °C during the day to −180 °C at night.
  • Despite being so close to the Sun, Mercury has ice in permanently shadowed craters at its poles.
  • Mercury has a very large iron core relative to its size, making up about 85% of its radius.
  • A day on Mercury (one full rotation) is longer than its year: 58.6 Earth days vs. 88 Earth days.
  • Mercury has a weak magnetic field, unusual for a small, rocky planet.
  • The planet has cliffs called “lobate scarps” that were formed as it cooled and contracted.
  • Mercury experiences “sunrise shadows” where craters at the poles remain in shadow even as the Sun rises, trapping ice.
