Mercury

The Smallest Wanderer

The closest planet to the Sun and the smallest in our solar system. A terrestrial planet with a solid, cratered surface that looks gray.

Venus

The Clouded Planet

Second from the Sun. Wrapped in thick creamy white and tan clouds, making it a terrestrial planet with a hidden surface.

Earth

Our Home Planet

Third from the Sun and fifth largest. The only known world with oceans of liquid water on its surface.

Mars

The Red Planet

Fourth from the Sun. Its distinctive color comes from iron minerals rusting in the soil. Home to deep canyons.

Jupiter

The Gas Giant

Fifth from the Sun and the largest planet. It has swirling gases, bands of color, and the famous Great Red Spot.

Saturn

The Ringed World

Sixth from the Sun. A massive gas giant famous for its spectacular, complex ring system made of ice and rock.

Uranus

The Ice Giant

Seventh from the Sun. Its pale blue color is caused by methane in its atmosphere. It is the only planet that spins on its side.

Neptune

The Distant Storm

Eighth from the Sun. An ice giant that is deep blue, banded with clouds, and known for supersonic winds.

About Dwarf Planets

Dwarf planets are celestial bodies that orbit the Sun and have sufficient mass for their self-gravity to overcome rigid body forces so that they assume a nearly round shape, but have not cleared their orbital neighborhood of other debris. The International Astronomical Union (IAU) currently recognizes five dwarf planets in our solar system: Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, and Eris.

Unlike the eight major planets, dwarf planets share their orbital space with other bodies of similar size. This distinction helps astronomers better classify the diverse objects in our solar system.

The Five Dwarfs

Pluto

The Beloved Dwarf

Reclassified in 2006. It has a famous heart-shaped glacier on its surface and five moons.

Ceres

King of the Belt

The only dwarf planet in the inner solar system. It resides in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

Haumea

The Oval Spinner

Located in the Kuiper Belt. It spins so fast that it has been pulled into the shape of an oval.

Makemake

The Bright One

Slightly smaller than Pluto, it is the second-brightest object in the Kuiper Belt.

Eris

The Disruptor

Its discovery triggered the debate that led to the official definition of "planet" and Pluto's reclassification.
